Lesson 8: How Likely Is It?

Lesson 8: How Likely Is It?

Objective:

You will understand the basic rules of probability. You will learn that previous outcomes do not give information about future outcomes if the events are independent.

Vocabulary:

probability, simulation, model, sample proportion, chance, independence

Essential Concepts:

Essential Concepts:

Probability is an area that we humans have poor intuition about. Probability measures a long-run proportion: 50% chance means the event happens 50% of the time if you repeated it forever. When we don't repeat forever, we see variability.

Lesson:

1.Consider possible synonyms to the word chance. If someone says, “That just happened by chance,” In your IDS journal, write down what "That happened by chance"" means

  1. Which game – chess or the board game “Sorry” – is more based on chance? Explain why

  2. Think of situations where chance is the only force at play. Write down a few situations where chance is the only force at play.

  3. Play the “Heads” video from the movie Rosencrantz and Guildenstern are Dead found at: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NbInZ5oJ0bc.

  4. In your IDS journals, write down your initial reactions to the clip by responding to the following questions:

    1. Is it possible to get 78 heads in a row when tossing a coin? Yes or no and explain

    2. Do you think it is likely to get 78 heads in a row? Yes or no and explain

    3. Write down how many times should we get a heads when tossing a coin?

    4. On average, write down how many times should the characters have gotten a heads out of the 78 tosses?

  5. Your teacher will give you an opportunity to share and discuss your results. Be prepared to discuss any differences and similarities you see.

  6. After discussing and comparing results, in your IDS Journal, write down any additions or revisions to your initial findings.

  7. The concept of chance, we can start learning about is called probability. Chance is simply the possibility that something will happen, and probability is a measurement for how often something happens in the “long run.” You may already have ideas about how to calculate probabilities based on prior classes or knowledge, but in IDS we will be taking a different approach – using simulations (see next step).

  8. Since we don’t want to actually flip a coin 78 times like the actors did in the video, we can have RStudio simulate them for us. A simulation is a way of creating random events that are close to real-life situations without actually doing them. It is a kind of model, which is a way of representing real world situations so that predictions can be made.

  9. R has a function that does coin flipping for us, and that it assumes an equal probability of heads and tails. In RStudio you will simulate a coin flip. Use the following function:

    > rflip(1)

  10. The value of 1 in the argument part of the function tells R to flip the coin 1 time. If you want to flip the coin 10 times, you could simply change the function to rflip(10).

  11. Run the function again using 10 as the number of times to flip the coin.

    1. Record the number of Heads (“H”s) you got

    2. Record the number of Tails (“T”s) you got

    3. In the output, explain what does Flipping 10 coins [Prob(Heads) = 0.5] mean?

    4. In the output, explain what does Number of Heads: 3 [Proportion Heads: 0.3] mean?

      Note: This is example output. Your sample may have a different value for the number of heads that appeared, and thus a different value for the proportion of heads.

  12. To relate back to the video at the beginning of the lesson, repeat the simulation once more, but use 78 as the number of coin flips rflip(78). Record answers to the following questions in the IDS journals:

    1. How many heads (“H”s) were there? Since we know to expect about 39 heads if the coin is fair, does the value seem reasonable?

    2. How many Tails (“T”s) were there?

    3. What proportion of the coin flips were heads?

  13. Using the rflip(78) command, run the simulation a few more times (3 – 5), record the values for the number of heads and the proportion of heads.

    As an example, we ran the function one times, look at the following value:

    Sample 1 – amount of heads: 45

            proportion of heads: 0.577

  14. Answer the questions listed below. The important thing to note is that the values can and (almost always) WILL change each time you run the simulation to create a new sample.

    1. How do the proportions of heads in the samples compare to each other?

    2. How do the proportions of heads compare to the true probability of heads (1/2 or 50%)?

    3. Why is there a 50% chance of getting heads during each coin flip?

  15. Next, answer following question in your IDS Journal:

    1. If you get a heads on the first toss of a coin, will you definitely get a heads on the next toss? Will you definitely get a tails on the next toss?

  16. When tossing a fair coin, there is no relationship between each toss. The second toss does NOT depend on the first toss; this concept is called independence. In this case,the coin tosses are independent of each other.

In your journal, write what you think the 3 most important topics were.

Homework

Create a Tweet (you do not have to post it online). Using 280 characters or fewer, write a Tweet about the meaning of probability.
